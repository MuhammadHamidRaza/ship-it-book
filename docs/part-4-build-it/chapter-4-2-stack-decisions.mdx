# Stack Decisions That Don't Trap You

You love technology. You want to use the newest framework, the fastest database, the most scalable architecture. Stop. Your stack decisions for an MVP should be boring.

The best technology for your first version is whatever lets you move the fastest. Not what's most popular. Not what scales to a million users. Not what looks best on your resume.

## The Boring Stack Philosophy

Use technologies you already know. If you know Python, use Django or FastAPI. If you know JavaScript, use Next.js or Express. Don't learn a new language for your MVP. Don't experiment with an unfamiliar database. Don't adopt a cutting-edge architecture.

Your goal is to ship, not to learn. You can learn new technologies when you have users and revenue. Right now, you have neither. Your only goal is to get both.

Here's my boring stack recommendation for a solo developer building an MVP:

- **Frontend**: React with Next.js or a single HTML page with HTMX
- **Backend**: Whatever language you know best
- **Database**: PostgreSQL (it scales further than you'll ever need)
- **Hosting**: Railway, Fly.io, or a $5 VPS
- **Auth**: A managed service like Clerk, Auth0, or Supabase
- **Payments**: Stripe

Every item on this list has been proven to work for thousands of startups. You don't need to innovate on infrastructure. You need to innovate on your product.

### Why Boring Wins

The "boring stack" is boring because it's predictable. You know exactly how it behaves. When something breaks, you can find the answer on Stack Overflow in seconds. When you need to hire help, there are thousands of developers who know this stack.

Compare that to a cutting-edge stack. You're using a framework released last month. You're the first person to combine these specific tools. When something breaks, there are no Stack Overflow answers. You are the Stack Overflow answer. The creator of the framework might respond to your issue in a few days. You're blocked until then.

The boring stack also reduces cognitive load. You don't need to think about your tools. You think about your product. The framework stays out of your way. You write code and it works.

I've seen developers spend two weeks setting up a Kubernetes cluster for an app with zero users. They were proud of the infrastructure. The app never shipped. Kubernetes was not the problem, but the obsession with infrastructure was a symptom of avoiding the real work: building something people want.

## Stack Migration Stories from Successful Startups

### Instagram's Django-to-Scale Journey

Instagram famously used Django and PostgreSQL. When they were acquired by Facebook, they had millions of users on a Python monolith. Critics said Django wouldn't scale. It scaled to hundreds of millions of users before they needed to migrate parts of the stack. And when they did migrate, they had the resources (money and engineers) to do it properly.

The lesson: Django was the right choice for Instagram's MVP. It let them ship fast. The scaling problems came later, after the product was validated. If they had tried to build a scalable architecture from day one, the product would have taken longer to ship and might never have found product-market fit.

### Twitter's Ruby-to-JVM Migration

Twitter started as a Ruby on Rails app. As traffic grew, Rails hit performance limits. They migrated critical paths to Scala on the JVM. The migration took years and required a team of talented engineers.

Here's the key insight: Twitter had millions of users before the migration. They had revenue. They had a team. They had the resources to handle a complex migration. If they had started with Scala, the MVP would have taken much longer and the company might not have survived long enough to have scaling problems.

Twitter's story is often cited as proof that Ruby doesn't scale. It actually proves the opposite: Ruby scaled well enough to get Twitter to millions of users and an acquisition offer. The migration was a later-stage luxury, not an early-stage necessity.

### GitHub's Rails Monolith

GitHub ran on a Rails monolith for over a decade. They served millions of developers, billions of requests, and stored terabytes of data on a single codebase that started as a simple Rails app. They did eventually break the monolith into services, but that happened years after the product was massively successful.

The lesson is consistent across these stories: successful startups optimize for speed of shipping first, and scalability second. The migration always comes after product-market fit.

## The Scale Trap

"You can't build on that, it won't scale."

Shut up. You don't have a scaling problem. You have a "zero users" problem. Scaling is a high-class problem that comes after product-market fit. Most startups die before they have ten users. Not one startup has died because they couldn't scale their MVP.

When you have ten thousand users, you can rewrite your backend. You'll have money and users. That's the best time to make architecture decisions. Not now.

The scale trap is seductive because it feels smart. "I'm thinking ahead. I'm being strategic." No, you're procrastinating. You're optimizing for a future that may never come. The most likely outcome for your startup is zero users, not a billion users. Optimize for the most likely outcome.

## How to Evaluate a Technology for Your Specific Context

Most developers evaluate technology based on popularity. "It's on Hacker News. The docs look good. It has a nice logo." This is not evaluation. This is vibes.

Here's a real evaluation framework. Score each technology on these criteria from 1 to 5.

**Familiarity**: How well do you already know this technology? 5 = you've shipped multiple projects with it. 1 = you've read a blog post.

**Community size**: How many developers use it? 5 = thousands of Stack Overflow answers, dozens of books. 1 = one GitHub repo with 100 stars.

**Documentation quality**: Can you find answers to common problems? 5 = official docs with examples, tutorials, and troubleshooting guides. 1 = a README with broken links.

**Deployment simplicity**: How long from commit to running in production? 5 = one command. 1 = a Dockerfile, a Kubernetes config, three cloud services, and a prayer.

**Migration cost**: If this is wrong, how hard is it to switch? 5 = swap one file. 1 = rewrite the entire codebase.

**Match to your problem**: Is this designed for what you're building? 5 = it's the standard solution. 1 = you're using a hammer on a screw.

Add up the scores. A technology with a total of 25-30 is a great choice. 15-24 is risky. Below 15 is dangerous.

Here's the trap: developers weight "match to problem" and "familiarity" low and "trendiness" high. Flip it. Familiarity and simplicity are the most important criteria for an MVP. You can optimize for perfect technology fit later.

## The "Debt Budget" Concept for Technical Decisions

Every technical decision creates technical debt. Some debt is worth taking. Some will bankrupt you. The key is knowing the difference.

Think of technical debt like a budget. You have a limited amount you can afford. You want to spend it on the decisions that matter and save it on the decisions that don't.

**Good debt**: Using a monolithic architecture instead of microservices. You'll need to split it later, but you saved months of upfront complexity. The debt has a clear payoff.

**Bad debt**: Not writing any tests. The debt doesn't save you time today. It slows you down immediately and compounds with every change. The payoff is zero.

**Neutral debt**: Using a third-party auth service instead of building your own. You'll pay a monthly fee and you're locked into their API. But you saved weeks of development. This is fine.

Here's how to apply the debt budget:

1. List every major technical decision for your MVP
2. For each one, estimate the cost of the "right" way and the cost of the "fast" way
3. Ask: "If I take the fast way, how much will it hurt to change later?"
4. If the migration cost is low, take the debt. If it's high, do it right.

For example, using raw SQL queries everywhere is bad debt because every schema change requires updating dozens of queries. Using an ORM (even a bad one) is good debt because the migration path is clear.

Skipping automated tests for the first two weeks is okay debt. Skipping tests forever is bankruptcy.

The debt budget keeps you honest. You're not ignoring quality. You're making intentional tradeoffs with a clear plan to pay them back.

## Decision Tree for Choosing Your MVP Stack

Ask these questions in order:

**Question 1**: What language do I know best?
→ Use that language. No exceptions. If you don't know any language well, learn JavaScript (most versatile for full-stack) or Python (fastest to prototype).

**Question 2**: Does the language have a well-established web framework?
→ If yes, use it. If no, pick the closest thing. A framework gives you routing, templating, database access, and authentication patterns out of the box. Don't build these yourself.

**Question 3**: Does the framework have a standard database integration?
→ Use that database. If the framework supports PostgreSQL (most do), use PostgreSQL. If it supports SQLite for development, that's fine too. Don't use MongoDB unless your data is genuinely document-structured (user-generated content, CMS). Don't use Cassandra or any distributed database.

**Question 4**: What can I outsource?
→ Auth, payments, email, file storage. Use managed services for all of these. Do not build them yourself. A managed auth service saves you weeks of security work. Stripe saves you months of PCI compliance. Managed email (SendGrid, Resend) saves you from spam deliverability nightmares.

**Question 5**: Where should I host?
→ The simplest option that works. Railway, Fly.io, or a $5 VPS. Do not use Kubernetes. Do not set up a CI/CD pipeline with Helm charts. You need a server that runs your code and a way to deploy updates. That's it.

**Question 6**: Am I done choosing?
→ Yes. Start building. If a choice turns out wrong, you'll know within a week. You can change it then. But you can't know what choices matter until you have real users.

Here's the decision tree in practice. A developer who knows Python well should use Django or FastAPI with PostgreSQL, hosted on Railway, with Auth0 for auth and Stripe for payments. This stack is boring. It's predictable. It works. The developer can ship in days instead of weeks.

A developer who insists on using Rust with a custom async runtime, ScyllaDB, and a self-hosted Kubernetes cluster will spend months on infrastructure. The product may never ship. And if it does, the Rust code won't be faster in a way users can perceive, because the bottleneck will be the product questions, not the execution speed.

## What to Optimize For

Optimize for:
- Speed of iteration: how fast can you make a change and ship it?
- Cognitive load: how much do you need to keep in your head?
- Deployment simplicity: how long from commit to production?
- Hiring ease (future you): can you find help if you get stuck?

Optimize against:
- "It's more scalable"
- "It's what big companies use"
- "It's the new hotness"
- "I want to learn this anyway"

## The Escape Plan

Every stack decision has an escape plan. Ask yourself: if this technology turns out to be the wrong choice, how hard is it to migrate?

A function that wraps your database calls can be swapped from SQLite to PostgreSQL. A service that abstracts your auth provider can switch from Auth0 to your own solution. But raw SQL embedded in your frontend code is a trap.

Design for migration. Use interfaces. Abstract your dependencies. Not because you'll need to migrate, but because the option gives you peace of mind to move fast.

## The Hard Truth

Your stack choice probably doesn't matter. Users don't care what framework you used. Investors don't care what database you chose. The market rewards products that solve problems, not codebases that use trendy technology.

The worst stack decision is not choosing unpopular technology. It's spending three weeks choosing your stack instead of shipping.

Pick something and build. If it turns out to be wrong, you'll know soon enough and you'll have learned what you actually need.

## Your Turn

Write down the stack you plan to use for your MVP. For each technology, answer:
1. Have I used this before?
2. Is this the fastest way to ship or the most interesting technology?
3. What is the escape plan if I need to switch?

Run the decision tree from this chapter against your stack. Score each technology using the evaluation framework. Apply the debt budget: which decisions are worth taking debt on and which aren't?

If you can't answer "yes" to question 1 and "fastest" to question 2, change your choice.

## What You Learned

- Use boring, familiar technology for your MVP
- Instagram, Twitter, and GitHub all scaled after shipping, not before
- Evaluate technology on familiarity, community, docs, and deployment simplicity
- You don't have a scaling problem, you have a users problem
- The debt budget helps you make intentional technical tradeoffs
- Optimize for iteration speed, not scalability
- Every dependency needs an escape plan
- Stack choice barely matters — shipping is everything
