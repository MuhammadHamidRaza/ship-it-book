# Twitter/X and LinkedIn Strategy

You're building a product. You need people to know about it. Social media is the cheapest way to reach potential users. But most developers use social media wrong.

They wait until launch day and post a link. Nobody clicks. They get discouraged and go back to building in silence. Here's a better approach.

## The Content Flywheel

Build your audience before you need it. Start posting about your building process months before your launch. Share what you're learning, the problems you're solving, the decisions you're making.

This creates a content flywheel:
1. You post valuable content about your building journey
2. People interested in your space follow you
3. You engage with them and build relationships
4. When you launch, your audience already cares
5. Your launch gets traction, which attracts more followers
6. You keep posting, which grows your audience further

The best time to start was six months ago. The second best time is today.

Let's look at the math. If you post once per day and each post gets you 2 followers, that's 60 followers per month. In six months, that's 360 followers. On launch day, if 5% of them visit your product, that's 18 visitors. If 10% of those sign up, that's 1-2 users from day one. This is not a viral success story. It's a sustainable, repeatable process that grows with time.

## The 1+3 Posting Rule

Here's a posting framework that balances creation with engagement: the 1+3 rule.

**Every day, you post 1 original piece of content and engage meaningfully with 3 posts from others.**

The 1 original post is your contribution to the conversation. Could be:
- A lesson you learned
- A screenshot of your progress
- A question to your audience
- A resource you found useful
- A hot take on your industry

The 3 engagements are how you build relationships:
- Reply to someone's tweet with a thoughtful comment (not just "great point" — add something)
- Quote-tweet someone's post with your take
- Share someone's article with your commentary

This rule ensures you're both creating and connecting. Pure broadcasting (posting without engaging) is a monologue. Pure engagement (replying without creating) builds no following. You need both.

## What to Post

Follow the 80/20 rule: 80% valuable content, 20% promotion. People follow you for value, not ads.

Valuable content includes:
- Lessons learned from building
- Technical decisions and why you made them
- Mistakes you made and what they taught you
- Screenshots of your progress (even ugly ones)
- Insights about your market or users
- Thoughts on building and startups
- Helpful resources you've discovered

Promotion includes:
- Product launches and updates
- Links to your product
- Calls to action

Post at least once a day. Consistency matters more than quality. One good tweet per day for a year is better than ten great tweets in a week followed by silence.

Here's a 30-day content calendar template for a builder:

```
Week 1: Foundation
Mon: Introduce yourself and what you're building
Tue: Share the problem you're solving (with a story)
Wed: Why existing solutions don't work for you
Thu: Your tech stack and why you chose it
Fri: Screenshot of your current progress (can be ugly)
Sat: A lesson you learned this week
Sun: Something non-work (hobby, book, or rest)

Week 2: Technical depth
Mon: A specific technical challenge you solved
Tue: Before/after refactoring comparison
Wed: Performance optimization you made
Thu: Database choice and tradeoffs
Fri: Deployment setup and what surprised you
Sat: Tool or library you discovered
Sun: Something non-work

Week 3: Market insights
Mon: What you learned from user research
Tue: Quote from a user interview (anonymized)
Wed: Your pricing strategy and why
Thu: Competitor analysis (respectful, not bashing)
Fri: Growth channel you're experimenting with
Sat: Metric you're tracking and why
Sun: Something non-work

Week 4: Launch prep
Mon: "I'm launching next week — here's what to expect"
Tue: Behind-the-scenes of your landing page design
Wed: The feature you're most proud of
Thu: A limitation you're aware of (transparency)
Fri: Launch thread (the big day!)
Sat: Launch results and what you learned
Sun: Thank you post to early supporters
```

The calendar removes the "what do I post today?" friction. Follow this for 30 days, then adjust based on what got the most engagement.

## Hashtag Strategy

Hashtags help people discover your content, but using the wrong ones marks you as a spammer.

**Twitter/X:** Hashtags are less important than they once were, but they still help in search. Use 1-3 relevant hashtags per post. Put them at the end, not inline.

Good hashtags for builders:
- #buildinpublic (the most relevant community)
- #indiehacker (for solo founders)
- #startup (general, broad reach)
- #SaaS (if you're building a SaaS)
- #[YourTechStack] (e.g., #React, #Python, #NodeJS)

Bad hashtags:
- #FollowForFollow — marks you as engagement-bait
- #EntrepreneurLife — vague, crowded
- #Success — meaningless
- #Marketing — too broad
- More than 5 hashtags — looks spammy

**LinkedIn:** Hashtags are more important here. Use 3-5 relevant hashtags per post. LinkedIn's algorithm uses them to categorize content and show it to relevant audiences.

Good LinkedIn hashtags for builders:
- #SaaS (active community)
- #Startup (professional audience)
- #Tech (broad reach)
- #[YourIndustry] (e.g., #FinTech, #HealthTech)
- #[YourSkill] (e.g., #FullStackDeveloper, #ProductDesign)

Create a saved list of 10-15 hashtags relevant to your niche. Rotate through them. Track which ones drive the most engagement.

## DM Outreach Scripts

Direct messages are a powerful but easily abused channel. Done right, they build relationships. Done wrong, they get you reported as spam.

DM outreach strategy: only DM people who have engaged with your content in the last 7 days. They already know who you are. They've already shown interest. The DM feels natural, not cold.

Script 1: Thanking an engager:
```
Hey [Name], thanks for the thoughtful reply on my post about [topic]. It made me think about [specific point they raised]. Are you building something in this space too?
```

Script 2: Asking for feedback (after they follow you):
```
Hey [Name], I noticed you followed after my post about [product]. I'm in the final stages of building and would love your perspective. Got 5 minutes for a quick question?
```

Script 3: Offering help:
```
Hey [Name], I saw your post about [their problem]. I dealt with something similar when building [your project]. Happy to share what I learned if it's helpful.
```

Script 4: Collaboration (when relevant):
```
Hey [Name], I've been following your work on [their project]. I'm building [your project] and think there could be an interesting crossover. Would you be open to a quick chat?
```

The golden rule of DMs: make it about them, not you. Every DM should answer "what's in it for them?" If you can't answer that, don't send it.

## LinkedIn Strategy

LinkedIn works better for B2B products and professional audiences. The culture is more formal but more supportive.

Publish long-form posts, not short updates. LinkedIn's algorithm favors posts that keep people on the platform. Posts with 500-1000 words that share a specific lesson or framework perform best.

Use a strong visual. A screenshot, a diagram, or a photo. Posts with images get significantly more engagement.

Connect with people after they engage with your post. Send a personalized message. "Thanks for engaging with my post about [topic]. I'm building [product] to solve [problem]. Would love to hear your thoughts."

LinkedIn post structure that works:

```
Headline: A specific, surprising claim or question

Hook (first 3 lines): The most important part. LinkedIn shows the first 3 lines before "see more." Make them count.

The story: A specific experience you had. Not general advice. "Last month, I [specific thing] and learned [specific lesson]."

The framework: 3-5 actionable points. Numbered or bulleted.

The takeaway: One sentence summary of what you want readers to remember.

Call to action: "What has your experience been?" or "Tag someone who needs to see this."
[1-3 relevant hashtags]
[Image: screenshot, diagram, or photo]
```

LinkedIn is a long-form platform. Your posts can be longer than tweets, but they should still be scannable. Use line breaks liberally. Short paragraphs. Bold key phrases (use bold text, not markdown — LinkedIn supports rich text).

## Twitter/X Strategy

Twitter is the best platform for developer audiences. The culture values authenticity and transparency.

Build a thread habit. Long-form threads get more engagement than single tweets. Tell stories. Share frameworks. Each tweet in the thread should make people want to read the next one.

Engage with others. Reply to tweets from people in your space. Share their content. Build genuine relationships. Twitter is a conversation, not a broadcast channel.

Use your bio effectively. Your bio should answer: who you are, what you're building, and why it matters. Include a link to your product or landing page.

Great bio template:

```
Building [product] to [solve problem] for [audience]
Previously [relevant experience]
[Link to product or landing page]
```

Example: "Building Feedly AI to help engineers stay current without information overload. Previously @Google Search. https://feedly.ai"

Keep it short. Twitter bios have a 160-character limit. Every word must earn its place.

## Engagement Strategies

Social media is social. Posting content is only half the equation. You must also engage.

**The 15-minute daily engagement block.** Every day, spend 15 minutes doing nothing but replying to and engaging with other people's content. Find 5 posts in your niche and leave thoughtful comments. Not "great post." Something that adds value.

**Build a "people to engage with" list.** Most social media platforms let you create lists. Create a list of 20-30 people in your space who consistently post valuable content. Check this list daily and engage with their latest posts. These relationships compound over time.

**The engagement tier system:**
- Tier 1 (daily): Reply to every comment on your posts
- Tier 2 (daily): Engage with 5 people in your niche
- Tier 3 (weekly): Share or quote-tweet 2-3 posts you found valuable
- Tier 4 (monthly): Write a thread highlighting other builders in your space

**Tagging strategy:** Tag people when it's genuinely relevant. "I used @tool to build this" is a great tag. "@influencer check out my product" with no context is noise. Tagging the subject of your post is good; tagging random people for visibility is bad.

## The Hard Truth

Social media is a long game. You won't gain 10,000 followers in a month. You'll gain 100 followers in three months. You'll feel like you're shouting into the void.

This is normal. Keep posting. The compound effect of consistent content is real. After six months, you'll have an audience. After a year, you'll have a community. After two years, people will find you.

The developers who succeed on social media are not the most talented. They're the most consistent.

The worst approach: posting for two weeks, getting discouraged by low engagement, quitting for two months, then coming back and wondering why you have no following. Consistency builds the algorithm's understanding of your content. Consistency builds your audience's expectation of your posting. Consistency builds your own habit. All three compound with time, not with intermittent bursts.

## Your Turn

Write five tweets about your building journey. They don't have to mention your product. They just have to be valuable to someone trying to solve the same problem you're working on.

Post one today. Schedule the others over the next week. After a week, evaluate: did anyone engage? What topics got the most response? Double down on those.

Create your 30-day content calendar using the template above. Commit to the 1+3 rule every day for the next 30 days. At the end of the month, review your analytics. How many new followers? How many engagements? What was your best performing post?

## What You Learned

- Build your audience before you need it with a content flywheel
- Follow the 1+3 rule: one original post, three engagements daily
- Use the 30-day content calendar to remove the "what do I post?" friction
- Use 1-3 relevant hashtags on Twitter, 3-5 on LinkedIn
- Follow DM outreach scripts that focus on the recipient, not you
- Twitter is best for developer audiences, LinkedIn for B2B
- Consistency beats occasional quality
- Social media is a long game — play it
