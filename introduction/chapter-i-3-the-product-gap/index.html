The Product Gap

There's a gap between what you know how to build and what the market needs. Most developers spend their careers on one side of this gap and wonder why their side projects never take off.

This is the product gap. It's the distance between a technically working application and a product that people actively seek out, use, and pay for.

The Two Sides of the Gap​

Side A: What you can build. You can build a full-stack application with authentication, a database, an API, and a responsive frontend. You can deploy it, monitor it, and scale it. Technically, it works.

Side B: What the market needs. A specific group of people has a specific problem that causes them pain, costs them money, or wastes their time. They are actively looking for a solution. They might be using a workaround that barely works.

The gap between A and B is where most developer-built products die.

Why the Gap Exists​

You built from your skills, not from the market. You asked "what can I build?" instead of "what should I build?"

When you build from your skills:

  • You choose a stack first, then look for a problem it solves
  • You build features that showcase your abilities
  • You optimize for code quality over user value
  • You focus on what's interesting to you, not what's needed by users

When you build from the market:

  • You start with a problem someone has
  • You choose the simplest solution that works
  • You optimize for user outcomes over technical elegance
  • You focus on what users need, even if it's boring to build

Closing the Gap​

Closing the product gap requires shifting from builder-centric thinking to user-centric thinking.

Step 1: Find the problem before the solution. Spend time in communities where your target users hang out. Read their complaints. Understand their workflows. A problem that people complain about repeatedly is a product opportunity.

Step 2: Solve one problem well. The biggest mistake developers make is trying to solve multiple problems at once. Pick one specific problem for one specific group of people. Solve it completely. Everything else is a distraction.

Step 3: Validate before you build. Before writing code, confirm that people actually want a solution. Talk to them. Show them a mockup. Get them to commit time or money. If they won't commit before you build, they won't commit after.

Step 4: Ship value, not features. Every release should make your product more valuable to your users. If a feature doesn't directly solve their problem, it doesn't belong in the next release.

The Technical Trap​

The product gap has a technical variant: building for technical impressiveness rather than user value.

You add WebSocket-based real-time updates because they're cool, not because users need them. You switch to a graph database because you want to learn it, not because your data model requires it. You rewrite your frontend in the latest framework because it's trendy, not because it improves the user experience.

Every technical decision should pass the test: "Does this make the product better for users?" If the answer is no, don't do it.

The Hard Truth​

You can write the most beautiful code in the world. If it doesn't solve a real problem for real people, it's not a product. It's a learning exercise.

The market does not care about your code. It cares about whether your product makes someone's life better. Bridge the product gap by starting with the problem, not the solution.

Your Turn​

Take one feature you're planning to build. Ask:

  1. What specific problem does this feature solve?
  2. Who has this problem?
  3. How do I know this is a real problem (not one I invented)?
  4. What is the simplest possible version of this feature?

If you can't answer all four questions, the feature is not ready to build.

End-of-Chapter Checklist​

  • I understand the product gap and why it exists
  • I've identified whether I tend to build from my skills or from the market
  • I can name the four steps to close the product gap
  • I've taken one planned feature and asked the four validation questions
  • I'm committed to starting with the problem, not the solution

What You Learned​

  • The product gap is the distance between what you can build and what the market needs
  • Building from your skills creates the gap; building from the market closes it
  • Closing the gap: find the problem → solve one thing well → validate → ship value
  • Avoid the technical trap: cool tech ≠ user value
  • The market rewards products that solve problems, not beautiful code

What's Next​

The product gap explains why your code doesn't always create value. But there's another force holding you back: the stories you've been told about startups. The next chapter debunks them.
